# Chapter 2 — The Market Problem and Regulatory Foundation

Digital asset succession faces an unprecedented challenge. While
traditional assets — real estate, securities, corporate holdings — have
been regulated for centuries through established legal mechanisms, a
growing portion of family wealth exists in spheres for which these
mechanisms were simply not designed. Cryptocurrencies, digital
identities, cloud assets, online businesses, NFTs, gaming assets,
creator-economy revenues, domains, and digital IP form a new asset
category whose succession planning is vastly underestimated by most
families. This chapter addresses that gap.

## 2.1 Digital Asset Amnesia

### The Invisible Asset

The fundamental problem of digital asset succession lies in a
paradoxical characteristic of modern digital assets: **they exist, but
they do not "remember" their origin, their entitled parties, or their
transfer rights**.

A physical gold treasure in a bank vault is embedded in a network of
legal recognitions through proof of ownership, wills, and inheritance
systems. A private key for cryptocurrencies, however, is merely a
cryptographic secret — without any connection to a legal identity,
without automatic transfer mechanisms, and without any memory of who
should own it.

If the owner of digital assets dies without having passed on their
access credentials, the assets continue to exist on the blockchain or
in cloud systems, but they are effectively lost. There is no heir to
claim them, no customer service to grant access, and no court that can
transfer the assets. The key is missing, and with it, everything is
lost. This situation is not hypothetical but daily reality.

### Numbers That Illustrate

Between 3 and 4 million Bitcoin have been estimated as permanently
lost — through forgotten passwords, defective hardware, or the death of
owners without succession planning.[^1] At current market values, this
represents losses in the hundreds of billions of euros. Yet Bitcoin is
only the most visible tip of a much larger iceberg.

Research predicts that by 2045, private digital assets worth over
**$6 trillion globally** will need to be inherited.[^2] This figure
includes cryptocurrencies, tokenized assets, digital art, online
businesses, social media identities with monetized revenue streams,
gaming assets, and asset categories that do not yet exist but will
emerge by then.

### The Human Dimension

Behind these abstract numbers lie concrete human fates. Families who
have built wealth through decades of work lose parts of this wealth
because of a lack of digital succession planning. The tragedy is that
this loss is avoidable. It does not arise from market fluctuations,
theft, or economic failure, but from a systemic failure of asset
planning that has simply been ignored.

A conventional will may be legally valid but offers no mechanism for
transferring digital assets. Access credentials are not automatically
released, cryptocurrencies cannot be withdrawn with an inheritance
certificate, and cloud assets exist in legal gray areas that challenge
even experienced lawyers.

> **Problem Box — The Invisible Loss**
> A family loses €2 million in cryptocurrencies because the deceased
> father never disclosed his seed phrase. The wealth exists — but no
> one can access it. No legal dispute, no lawsuit, no intervention can
> reconstruct the key. The money is literally lost.

## 2.2 Regulatory Reality in Europe

### BGH Ruling III ZR 183/17 and Its Implications

The legal landscape of digital asset succession in Germany was
fundamentally shaped by the Federal Court of Justice (BGH) ruling
**III ZR 183/17** of July 12, 2018.[^3] The ruling clarified that even
for digital assets that do not constitute classic property rights,
inheritance-related disclosure obligations exist. Heirs have a right to
information about the deceased's digital assets — regardless of whether
the service provider custodies physical assets.

Many institutions assumed that a "no-custody" model — merely providing
infrastructure without direct custody — would minimize liability. The
BGH ruling made clear that this assumption is incorrect. **Those
enabling digital inheritance scenarios assume information obligations —
regardless of whether they custody assets or not.**

Financial institutions cannot simply claim they have "no customer
assets" and are therefore unaffected. The mere existence of a user
account with digital assets triggers documentation and information
obligations. Non-fulfillment risks liability claims and reputational
damage.

### The MiCA Regulation

The European **Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA, EU 2023/1114)**,
fully applicable since 2024[^4], creates a harmonized legal framework
for crypto-assets across the EU. While MiCA primarily addresses consumer
protection and market integrity, it contains far-reaching requirements
for the documentation, custody, and transfer of digital assets.

Particularly relevant for succession are the requirements for
whitepaper obligations, due diligence toward investors, and transparent
presentation of all relevant information. These obligations extend not
only to the original investment moment but potentially also to the
transfer point. Institutions offering or managing digital assets must
have mechanisms that enable proper transfer — whether during the
owner's lifetime or upon death.

### BaFin Supervisory Practice

The German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (**BaFin**) has
continuously tightened its supervisory practice since the BGH ruling
and MiCA's introduction. Requirements for documenting digital assets,
tracing transactions, and cooperating with heirs have significantly
increased. Institutions managing digital assets must maintain extensive
evidence of ownership, access rights, and transfer mechanisms.

This tightening is not limited to Germany. In Austria, the Financial
Market Authority (**FMA**) has established comparable requirements; in
Switzerland, **FINMA** has continuously expanded its guidelines for
digital assets. For cross-border institutions, this means considering
the strictest requirements — a multiplier for compliance costs and
complexity.

### International Harmonization

International comparison shows a fragmented but harmonizing landscape.
The **Financial Action Task Force (FATF)** has developed guidelines for
digital assets adopted by most jurisdictions.[^5] The **OECD** is
working on standards for taxing digital assets.[^6] Post-Brexit, the UK
has developed approaches closely inspired by EU requirements.

> **Regulatory Fact — Documentation Obligations**
> Under MiCA and expanded BaFin supervisory practice, institutions must
> apply the same documentation standards to digital assets as to
> traditional assets: identification of beneficial owners,
> traceability of transfers, and information provision to entitled
> third parties — including heirs.

## 2.3 Liability Risks for Financial Institutions and Advisors

### Information Obligations Toward Heirs

Recent legal developments send a clear message: financial institutions
cannot free themselves from information obligations through "no-custody"
models or technical distancing. Those providing digital infrastructures,
managing access capabilities, or operating technical interfaces for
digital assets assume obligations that become relevant in inheritance.

These obligations go beyond merely providing information. Institutions
must be able to substantively answer legitimate inquiries from heirs:
What digital assets existed? What access mechanisms were intended?
What transfer options existed? Those without corresponding structures
risk both civil claims and supervisory consequences.

### Process Costs and Economic Burden

Unstructured inheritance cases carry significant financial consequences.
Litigation costs for heirs enforcing their rights can reach
**€50,000–200,000** depending on complexity. These costs can fall back
on the managing institution if it has violated its obligations.
Additionally, internal processing of inheritance cases without
structured processes, clear responsibilities, and corresponding
infrastructure ties up significant resources.

### Personal Liability of Decision-Makers

An aspect often underestimated is the **personal liability** of
decision-makers. Managing directors, board members, and compliance
officers can be held personally liable for breaches of duty in digital
asset succession — particularly when it was known that action was
needed but no measures were taken.

Case law in recent years has shown an increasing tendency toward
personal liability of decision-makers who remain inactive despite
clear regulatory requirements. In digital asset succession, where
legal requirements are established (BGH ruling, MiCA), the argument
of ignorance is not tenable.

> **For C-Level Decision-Makers**
> Personal liability risk is real and significant. Decision-makers
> must ensure their institution has adequate structures for digital
> asset succession — not only for strategic reasons but for their
> own protection.

## 2.4 The Demographic Pressure Wave

The baby-boomer generation, born 1946–1964, reaches typical inheritance
age between 2025 and 2035. This generation has built both traditional
wealth and, in many cases, significant digital assets — through early
cryptocurrency investments, digital businesses, or accumulated digital
identities.

Studies show that **15–20% of cryptocurrency wealth is held by persons
over 50**.[^7] This number will rise dramatically as the generation of
digital natives advances into this age group. Demand for professional
digital succession planning will therefore grow not linearly but
exponentially.

The market is massively underserved. Traditional financial service
providers have long ignored digital-asset succession planning.
Specialized providers focus on the consumer market and do not serve
institutional needs. **This supply gap creates a first-mover
opportunity.**

> **Problem Box — The Window Is Closing**
> A family office with 50 clients expects 30–40 inheritance cases in
> the next 10 years. Without structured digital succession planning,
> these are 30–40 potential problem cases with uncertain outcomes.

## 2.5 Competitive Landscape in the B2B Segment

The current market is dominated by consumer-focused providers —
**Trust & Will**, **Everplans**, and a growing number of startups
targeting individual consumers with digital-will platforms.

These providers serve an important market but do not address the
specific needs of professional market participants. Their solutions
are tailored to individual users, not institutional requirements. They
offer no interfaces for integration into existing wealth structures,
no compliance documentation for supervisory purposes, and no
mechanisms for scaled processing.

**The real opportunity lies in the gap between consumer tools and
institutional infrastructure.** Family offices, asset managers, and
advisory firms need solutions that integrate into existing processes,
meet regulatory requirements, and enable professional, scalable
processing. This gap is not served today.

## 2.6 Synthesis: The Dual Persuasion Power

The combination of emotional resonance and rational foundation creates
a persuasive power that could not be achieved through purely factual
or purely emotional means.

On the **emotional level**: fear of loss — families losing wealth,
heirs facing closed doors, assets disappearing. This fear is justified
and corresponds to daily market experience.

On the **rational level**: regulatory requirements, liability risks,
and demographic development. These factors are objectively measurable
and unavoidable. The regulatory landscape will not become simpler,
liability risks will not decrease, and demographic development will
not reverse.

## Summary for Practice

1. The problem of digital asset succession is **acute and growing
   exponentially**.
2. Regulatory developments (**BGH ruling, MiCA**) create clear
   obligations with significant non-compliance consequences.
3. **Liability risks for decision-makers are real and personal**.
4. Demographic development creates temporal pressure requiring
   proactive action.
5. The competitive landscape offers a **favorable first-mover
   opportunity** in the B2B segment.
